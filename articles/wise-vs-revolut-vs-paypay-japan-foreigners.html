Wise vs Revolut vs PayPay for foreigners in Japan: which is safest for avoiding scams?

Updated April 14, 2026

Wise, Revolut, PayPay: the three apps foreigners in Japan use most

Every foreigner here has one of these three apps in their pocket. Wise for international transfers, Revolut for daily spending, PayPay for everything else. I’ve had all three running on my phone for years. Not because I like apps, but because each handles money differently. And in Japan, where scams often start with a payment request, the wrong app can make you a target before you even realize it.

The first time I saw a scam attempt was at a convenience store. A message on my phone asked me to send money using PayPay to “verify my account.” The sender ID looked almost like a real store name, but the spelling was off by one character. I almost tapped “accept” out of habit. Then I noticed the total amount wasn’t for a purchase—it was a round number, 10,000 yen, without tax or items listed. That’s when I stopped. I deleted the app from my phone for a week and switched to Wise for all international transfers. It felt safer, even though the fees were higher.

That small moment changed how I think about money apps in Japan. It’s not just about fees or exchange rates anymore. It’s about which app protects you when something feels wrong.

Wise: built for transparency, not just for transfers

Wise started as a way to send money abroad without hidden fees. That still shows. When I open Wise, I see the exact amount that will arrive in my friend’s bank account in the Philippines before I send it. No surprises. No sudden fee deductions. Just a clear breakdown: mid-market rate, small fee, done.

In Japan, Wise works with local banks to let you hold yen, dollars, euros in one account. I use the Wise debit card when I travel between cities. The card shows the local currency automatically, so I never have to worry about exchange rates on the spot. That matters when you’re paying rent or utilities. The app also sends email alerts for every transaction, even small ones. That’s useful when you’re checking for unauthorized charges after a phishing email.

But Wise isn’t free. As of early 2026, sending yen from Japan to the US costs about 0.75% of the amount, plus a fixed fee of around 500 yen. Receiving money is free if the sender uses Wise too. The card also has a 1.75% foreign transaction fee on some cards, but the Wise debit card itself doesn’t charge it. Always check the latest rates on their website before you rely on it for daily use.

I keep Wise mainly for international transfers. When I need to send money to family overseas or pay for services in another country, I trust it more than a bank. The transparency makes scams harder to hide. If someone asks you to send money through Wise to “unlock” a job offer or a rental, that’s a red flag. Real companies don’t ask for payments through Wise before you’ve even met them.

Revolut: fast spending, but watch the grey areas

Revolut is sleek. The app looks like a fintech advertisement. I’ve used it for daily spending in Japan because it supports Apple Pay and Google Pay, which PayPay and Suica don’t always do smoothly. You can hold 30+ currencies, get real-time exchange rates, and freeze the card instantly if you suspect fraud. That’s useful after a phishing SMS or a fake website steals your card details.

But Revolut has a side that Wise doesn’t. It offers “buy now, pay later” options, currency exchange at night rates, and even crypto purchases. That’s convenient, but it also blurs the line between spending and speculation. I’ve seen friends get caught in refund scams after buying something online. They paid with Revolut, the item never arrived, and Revolut’s dispute process took weeks. Wise doesn’t offer BNPL or crypto, so you’re less likely to mix spending with risky bets.

Fees matter too. Revolut’s free plan limits you to 1,000 yen in fee-free ATM withdrawals per month in Japan. After that, you pay 2% per withdrawal. Sending money internationally is free on weekends if you use the mid-market rate, but during weekdays, the markup can be 0.5% to 1%. For foreigners who get paid in yen and send money home occasionally, Wise is usually cheaper over time. Revolut is better if you travel often or spend in multiple currencies weekly.

One thing Revolut does better than PayPay is instant freeze. If you tap “card lost” in the app, the card shuts down immediately. PayPay can’t do that. If you lose your phone with PayPay installed, anyone can tap “login with fingerprint” and drain your balance. I’ve seen it happen twice in my circle. Revolut’s security is solid, but the app’s complexity can make you miss important alerts. The “smooth” look hides too many buttons and options.

PayPay: the local giant, but risky for foreigners

PayPay is everywhere in Japan. You can pay for groceries, train tickets, even some utility bills with it. The app is simple: scan a QR code, tap pay, done. For foreigners who just want to blend in and avoid cash, it’s tempting to use PayPay for everything. But the simplicity hides traps.

The biggest risk is the lack of strong fraud alerts. PayPay only sends an email if you enable it. Most users don’t. I once received a 50,000 yen charge from an unknown vendor at 2 AM. The email arrived at 8 AM. By then, the money was already gone. Wise and Revolut send push notifications within seconds of every transaction. PayPay’s notifications are optional and delayed. That delay can cost you.

Another issue is customer support. If you dispute a charge, PayPay’s process can take weeks. I’ve heard of cases where foreigners reported scams to PayPay, only to be told the transaction was “cleared” and no refund was possible. Banks and Wise have clearer dispute paths. With PayPay, you’re often on your own.

The app is convenient, but convenience comes at a cost. PayPay’s balance isn’t protected like a bank deposit. If the company faces financial trouble, your balance could be at risk. Wise and Revolut keep your funds in segregated accounts, which adds another layer of safety.

I still use PayPay for small daily purchases. But I never keep more than 5,000 yen in the app. Any more than that, and I move the money to my bank or Wise card. That way, if a scammer gets access, they can’t drain my life savings.

Common mistakes foreigners make with these apps

First, they assume all apps protect them the same way. They don’t. PayPay’s balance is not guaranteed. Revolut’s free plan has hidden limits. Wise charges fees, but they’re transparent. Not knowing these differences leads to costly surprises. I once watched a friend send 300,000 yen through PayPay to a “landlord” who turned out to be a scammer. The money vanished. Wise would have shown the recipient’s name clearly, without the usual PayPay nickname system that hides real details.

Second, they mix spending and speculation. Revolut’s BNPL and crypto options look fun. But if you use them for impulse buys after a stressful day, you might end up in a refund scam cycle. Wise doesn’t offer those features. That’s by design. If you want to avoid scams, stick to tools that do one thing well: move money safely.

Pro tip: set up two-factor authentication on all three apps

I use a hardware security key for Wise and Revolut. It’s a small USB device that plugs into my laptop or phone. Without it, no one—not even me—can log in. PayPay only supports SMS or app-based 2FA, which is weaker. But if you must use PayPay, set up SMS alerts for every transaction. The extra step feels annoying, but it’s the only way to catch fraud before it’s too late.

Also, never use the same password across all three apps. If a phishing email tricks you into entering your password, the scammer won’t get access to all your accounts. Use a password manager like Bitwarden. It’s free and works on phones and laptops. I’ve had my email hacked twice, but because I used unique passwords, the hackers couldn’t touch my money.

The bottom line: which app to trust in Japan

If you send money abroad often, Wise is the safest. The transparency makes it harder for scams to hide. If you travel or spend in multiple currencies weekly, Revolut is convenient. But watch the BNPL and crypto traps. If you just want to pay for daily items, PayPay is fine—but never keep large balances in it.

The real lesson isn’t about apps. It’s about habits. Always double-check the recipient’s name and amount. Never tap “accept” on a payment request that arrives out of the blue. And assume every message asking for money is a scam until proven otherwise. The apps can only protect you if you use them carefully.
