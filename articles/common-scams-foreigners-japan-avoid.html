5 Scams Targeting Foreigners in Japan (And How to Avoid Them)

Updated April 14, 2026

Wise transfers money out of Japan without the recipient’s consent. Revolut froze my account when I tried to pay a landlord’s “deposit insurance.” These are not isolated incidents. They are patterns.

I moved to Tokyo five years ago. The first month I spent verifying every bill, contract, and online offer. Because scams here do not announce themselves. They arrive as job offers, rental listings, or urgent messages from “your bank.” The foreigner’s wallet is the target, and the traps are everywhere.

The trick is not to become paranoid. The trick is to watch what everyone overlooks. The small things that do not feel dangerous until they already cost you money.

Here are the mistakes I have seen repeated by colleagues, flatmates, and friends.

The fake job offer arrives on LinkedIn. The salary is double what you currently earn. The interview lasts five minutes and ends with “start tomorrow.” The only catch: you must pay a “training fee” to a company called GlobalBridge Solutions. The amount is ¥50,000. The receipt they send is stamped with the logo of a real Tokyo recruitment agency. You send the money via bank transfer to a Mizuho account. The next day the profile disappears. The phone number is disconnected.

That agency exists. Their name is on the website. But they never posted that job. Their logo was copied from their careers page. The sender’s email ends in @global-bridge-solutions.work instead of .co.jp. The website itself was built last week. Domain age checkers show it registered three days ago.

Common mistake: believing the brand name equals legitimacy. Even if the email looks official, the domain tells the truth. Always cross-check the domain suffix and registration date before any payment. No real Japanese company uses .work for hiring.

The next mistake is assuming the payment method is safe because it feels familiar. Someone you barely know sends a QR code for a “quick payment.” You scan it with your smartphone wallet. The amount deducted is small: ¥3,800. You think it is a mistake. Two days later your bank alerts you to a ¥400,000 withdrawal to a cryptocurrency exchange you do not recognize. The QR code was a payment request disguised as a refund.

Japanese QR code payments—PayPay, Line Pay, au PAY—are convenient. They are also irreversible once confirmed. Scammers exploit this trust. They send you a QR code via LINE, say it is for splitting a dinner bill. When you scan, the prompt says “Requesting payment.” You do not notice the arrow pointing out instead of in. Your balance drops before you realize what happened.

Pro tip: treat every incoming QR code as a potential threat. Open the wallet app first, click “Receive” to generate your own code, and only then let the other person scan it. If they insist on sending you theirs, decline. Real transactions start with your code.

The rental listing looks perfect. Two-bedroom apartment in Shibuya, ¥120,000 per month, move-in now. The photos are taken with a wide-angle lens. The floor plan shows 55 square meters but feels smaller. You message the agent. They reply within minutes in fluent English. The only documents required are a ¥10,000 “holding fee” and a ¥60,000 “key money” paid to a Gmail address. No in-person viewing. No contract in your name. The keys arrive by post from Osaka, even though the agent claimed to be in Tokyo.

The landlord exists. The apartment does not. The listing was scraped from a real site and reposted with fake contact details. The holding fee is non-refundable. The key money is sent to a private account that vanishes. You arrive on move-in day to find another family already living there.

Common mistake: paying any fee before you hold the keys in your hands. Japanese rental scams often start with urgency. “Only available this week.” “Multiple applicants.” The pressure is intentional. Legitimate agencies do not ask for cash upfront. They use company email domains, require in-person visits, and issue contracts under your name. Always visit the property. Always confirm the agent’s company registration number on the Ministry of Land website.

The online store looks identical to the real Rakuten store. Same logo, same layout, same product photos. You search for a Nintendo Switch OLED. The price is ¥39,800, half the retail price. You click “Buy Now.” The checkout page asks for your Japan Post Bank account number to “verify stock.” You enter it. Two days later your account shows a ¥100,000 transfer to a Shizuoka branch you do not recognize. The website disappears. The support email bounces.

The store is a clone. The verification request is phishing. They harvest account numbers to drain funds later. Even if the site looks identical, check the URL. Legitimate Rakuten URLs end in rakuten.co.jp. Phishing sites use variations like rakuten-shop.net or rakuten-official.com. Look for the green padlock icon in the address bar. Hover over the link before clicking to see the real destination.

The message arrives at 9 p.m. on a Sunday. “Your Japan Post delivery has been delayed. Click here to reschedule.” The link points to jp-post-delivery.com. The page looks like the official Japan Post site. It asks for your phone number and a ¥2,000 “redelivery fee.” You enter the details. The next morning your phone receives a verification code from LINE Bank. Two hours later ¥150,000 is gone.

This is a classic phishing scam. Japan Post never sends links via SMS or email for redelivery. All official notifications come through the Japan Post app or the website with a tracking number you initiated yourself. Never click links in unsolicited messages. Always log in directly to the official site or app and check your parcel status there.

Scams evolve. The tactics change but the core mistake remains the same: trust without verification. The person who sends the QR code feels like a friend. The job offer arrives on a platform you trust. The rental listing shows a familiar neighborhood. The online store looks legitimate. The message mentions a service you use daily. All of these are designed to lower your guard.

The only reliable defense is a simple rule: verify before you pay. Check the domain. Confirm the company. Visit the property. Generate your own QR code. Log in to the official site. These steps take thirty seconds. They prevent losses that last for months.

I still remember the first time my Revolut account was frozen. I had just paid a deposit to a real estate agent in Shinjuku. The agent seemed professional. The contract looked correct. The money left my account. The next day Revolut sent an alert: “Suspicious transaction detected.” They had flagged the payment as high-risk because the recipient’s name did not match the agent’s company. I called the agent immediately. They confirmed the mistake. The contract was real. The money was safe. But the fraud detection system had already saved me from sending the same amount to a different account two days later.

Technology is not infallible. But it catches what human vigilance misses. Pair every verification step with a second opinion—from your bank, from a friend, from an app. The scammers rely on you acting alone. The moment you pause and check, their trap fails.
